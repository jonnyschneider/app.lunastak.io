## Observations

### 1. Document Extraction Needs Work

**What happened:** Uploaded a competitor analysis conversation from Perplexity. Lunastak processed the document "as is" rather than understanding its purpose in context. The system didn't grasp that this was competitor intelligence to inform *our* strategy—it just saw content.

**The gap:** Documents lack contextual framing. Why did the user upload this? What should Lunastak extract from it?

**Potential solutions:**
- **Narrative prompt on upload:** Ask the user to briefly explain why they're uploading the document. What insight are they hoping to capture?
- **Proper document model:** Move beyond documents as "conversation starters" toward a richer model where documents are first-class entities with:
  - Synthesis and extracted insights
  - User notes and corrections
  - Related conversations
  - Version history

**Current limitation:** Document upload is really just a cold-start workaround—not a genuine document management capability. This is fine for MVP, but the seams are showing.

---

### 2. Synthesis/Summary Quality: ✓ Strong

**What worked:** The synthesis captured the essence of the information well and at the right altitude. The narrative summaries for each emergent theme felt appropriately pitched—not too detailed, not too abstract.

**Trust signal:** This is where Lunastak started to earn trust. The feeling of "yes, we're getting somewhere here" is exactly what we want. The system demonstrated it understood the material.

**Implication:** The extraction-to-synthesis pipeline is working. Protect this.

---

### 3. Conversation Follow-ups: ✓ Surprisingly Good

**What happened:** Continued the exploration through two additional questions. The follow-ups were well-targeted and felt natural.

**Interesting observation:** This variant wasn't using the new 11-dimension schema, yet coverage felt comprehensive without being formulaic or forced.

**Concern worth noting:** It's possible this emergent, less-structured approach actually produces better conversation flow than the forthcoming dimension-driven model (releasing behind feature flag). Worth monitoring carefully when the new model ships.

---

### 4. Decision Stack Output: Mixed

**What worked:** The output was useful and structurally sound.

**What didn't:** Felt a little disappointing. The fundamental challenge: communicating complicated strategic ideas in pithy, memorable ways while:
- Making leaders feel their unsaid complexities are acknowledged
- Remaining accessible to everyone in the organisation

This balance is incredibly difficult. Getting it right is the difference between a strategy that lives on the wall and one that lives in a drawer.

**Potential solutions:**

**UI-level iteration:**
- Allow manual editing of stack items directly in the UI
- Enable critique-and-refine workflows—launch into collaborative adjustment rounds
- Support versioning (iterate toward v2, v3 of the stack)
- Could be whole-stack refinement in a new conversation thread, or per-item adjustment

**Devil's Advocate mode:**
- Optional capability to tear the strategy apart
- Some leaders would love this; others would find it demoralising
- Build as an optional sub-agent or skill
- Not for the faint-hearted—needs careful positioning

---

### 5. Objectives & Metrics: ✗ Needs Significant Work

**Known issues confirmed:**
- **Dates completely wrong:** Referencing 2023 when it's 2026
- **Metric:trend mismatches:** e.g., "15% increase" when the metric should decrease (revenue churn, for instance)

**Fix required:** Rules and tuning around:
- Temporal awareness (current date context)
- Metric polarity (which direction is "good" for each metric type)
- Sanity checking of generated targets

---

### 6. Altitude Tension: Realistic vs. Aspirational

**The puzzle:** Vision, Strategy, and Objectives were technically correct, reasonable, and accurate. Goals referenced problem-solution fit, 20 paying customers, etc. Realistic for the stage.

**But:** It didn't feel aspirational enough. Too much "ops doc", not enough inspiration.

**Why this matters:** Leaders will dismiss boring strategy documents. The whole artefact needs to strike a chord—make them want to share it, talk about it, take it out of the drawer.

**The tension to solve:**
- Realistic enough to be credible and actionable
- Stretch/aspirational enough to energise and inspire
- If it reads like a task list, we've failed

**Design challenge:** How do we help users calibrate altitude? Some will want grounded realism; others need permission to dream bigger. The system needs to flex.

## Reflections

The core value proposition is visible: Lunastak *can* capture fragmented strategic thinking and synthesise it meaningfully. The synthesis quality and conversation follow-ups demonstrate genuine understanding.

But the output layer—where strategy becomes artefact—still feels too safe, too generic, too operational. The Decision Stack should feel like a manifesto, not a planning document.

The tension between "accurate reflection of inputs" and "inspiring strategic narrative" is where the real product work lies.
